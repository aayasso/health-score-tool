## Section: What Is the Health Environment Score?

The Health Environment Score measures the environmental conditions in your neighborhood that influence health outcomes over time. It synthesizes data from seven federal agencies into a single composite score (0-100) and letter grade (A through F) for each ZIP code.

Unlike tools that measure walkability or built environment convenience, the Health Environment Score measures health environment *outcomes* -- the air you breathe, the noise you live with, the food you can access, and the climate resilience of your surroundings. Each ZIP code receives scores across five dimensions, plus an overall composite.

The score is built entirely from authoritative federal data sources. No surveys, no self-reported data, no proprietary inputs. Every score can be traced back to publicly available datasets from the CDC, EPA, USDA, NASA, and other federal agencies.

## Section: Five Dimensions of Neighborhood Health

**Section intro:** Each ZIP code is scored across five interlocking dimensions. Together, they capture the full environmental context that shapes health outcomes.

---

### Respiratory Health Score
*Air quality, pollution exposure, green space, and respiratory disease rates*

| Component | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| Air Quality | Outdoor air pollution levels including fine particulate matter, ozone, and nitrogen dioxide |
| Environmental Burden | Industrial and traffic exposure -- proximity to diesel corridors, industrial facilities, and other pollution sources |
| Green Cover | Tree canopy and vegetation -- the share of the neighborhood covered by trees and green space that filter pollutants |
| Health Outcomes | Local respiratory disease rates including asthma and COPD prevalence |

### Cardiovascular Health Score
*Physical activity, heart disease, noise exposure, and neighborhood walkability*

| Component | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| Physical Inactivity | The share of adults reporting no leisure-time physical activity |
| Heart Disease Prevalence | Coronary heart disease rates among residents |
| Transportation Noise | Highway and aviation noise exposure linked to elevated blood pressure and cardiovascular events |
| Walkability / Surface Cover | Impervious surface coverage -- roads, parking lots, and rooftops that signal car-dependent design |

### Stress & Sensory Environment Score
*Noise, light pollution, depression, and mental health burden*

| Component | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| Transportation Noise | Highway and aviation noise that disrupts sleep and increases stress hormones |
| Light Pollution | Nighttime artificial light intensity that disrupts circadian rhythms and impairs sleep quality |
| Depression Prevalence | Local depression rates among residents |
| Poor Mental Health Days | Self-reported frequency of poor mental health days |

### Food Access Score
*Supermarket proximity, grocery availability, and diet-related health outcomes*

| Component | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| Supermarket Access | The share of the population within walking distance of a supermarket |
| Grocery Store Availability | Grocery stores per capita in the local area |
| Diet-Related Health | Diabetes and obesity prevalence among residents |

### Heat & Climate Resilience Score
*Tree canopy, pavement coverage, and heat-sensitive health conditions*

| Component | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| Tree Canopy & Shade | The percentage of the neighborhood shaded by trees -- the most effective natural defense against urban heat |
| Pavement & Built Surface | Impervious surface coverage -- roads, rooftops, and parking lots that absorb and radiate heat |
| Heat-Sensitive Health | Asthma and COPD prevalence -- respiratory conditions directly worsened by heat exposure |

## Section: Grade Scale

| Grade | Score Range | Label | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | 80-100 | Excellent | Among the strongest health environments in the coverage area. Environmental conditions broadly support long-term health. |
| B | 65-79 | Good | Above-average conditions across most dimensions. Minor concerns may exist in specific areas. |
| C | 50-64 | Fair | Moderate conditions. Some environmental factors warrant attention, particularly for sensitive populations. |
| D | 35-49 | Poor | Below-average conditions. Multiple environmental factors present meaningful health risks. |
| F | Below 35 | Very Poor | Significant environmental health challenges across multiple dimensions. |

## Section: Federal Data Sources

**Section intro:** Every score is derived from publicly available federal datasets. No proprietary data, no paid licenses, no surveys.

| Source | Agency | What It Provides |
|---|---|---|
| CDC PLACES | Centers for Disease Control and Prevention | ZIP-level chronic disease prevalence, mental health indicators, and health behavior estimates |
| EPA AQS | Environmental Protection Agency | Air quality monitoring data -- PM2.5, ozone, and NO2 concentrations from stations nationwide |
| EPA EJScreen | Environmental Protection Agency | Environmental justice screening data -- cumulative pollution burden and demographic vulnerability indicators |
| NLCD | U.S. Geological Survey | National Land Cover Database -- satellite-derived tree canopy coverage and impervious surface measurements |
| BTS Noise Map | Bureau of Transportation Statistics | Modeled day-night average sound levels from highways, railways, and aviation |
| NASA VIIRS | NASA | Satellite-observed nighttime light intensity measuring artificial light pollution |
| USDA Food Access Research Atlas | U.S. Department of Agriculture | Census tract-level data on population proximity to supermarkets and grocery stores |
| USDA Food Environment Atlas | U.S. Department of Agriculture | County-level food environment indicators including grocery store availability |

## Section: How Scores Are Calculated (methodology note)

Each ZIP code is scored on a 0-100 scale across five dimensions. Scores are derived from multiple federal data inputs per dimension, normalized to a common scale, and combined into a composite score. Letter grades are assigned based on fixed thresholds (A through F).

All scores use the same grading scale and are comparable across ZIP codes and metros. Scores are dated and will be refreshed annually to enable longitudinal tracking of neighborhood health environment trends.

The scoring methodology -- including component weighting, normalization approach, and composite logic -- is proprietary to LaSalle Technologies.
